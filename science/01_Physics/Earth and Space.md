> Earths movement or orbit around the sun or the solar system, is defined by two cycles: A daily rotation of $360^\circ$ and an annual orbit around the sun. While it feels like we are standing still, Earth is in constant motion.


# Solar System Models

Back then, the people used the Geocentric model of the solar system (Ptolemy), it was accepted for 1500 years as there were no measurement/observation tool that could be used. This theory theorized that the Earth was at the center of the solar system rather than the sun.

The Heliocentric model was found through thorough mathematical observation and basic telescopes. Copernicus and Galileo popularized this new heliocentric model, and this model was later refined by [[Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion]], which states that planets move in ellipses (not circles). It also states that the sun is not really at "the center", but instead at a focal point of the elliptical orbit, and the speed of the planets are not constant. This is the current model for the solar system that we use in the modern world.

# Earth's Seasons

### The Core Variable: Axial Tilt

The primary cause of seasons is **not distance**, but the **$23.5^\circ$ tilt** (or more accurately $23.44^\circ$) of Earth’s axis relative to its orbital plane. This tilt stays the same as it orbits and rotates around the sun.
- **Tilt Toward Sun:** Concentrated rays + Longer duration = **Summer**.
- **Tilt Away from Sun:** Spread-out rays + Shorter duration = **Winter**.

### The Logic of Solar Intensity

- **Concentrated Heat (High Sun Angle):** Solar energy hits a small area directly. High efficiency $\rightarrow$ High Heat.
- **Spread Out Rays (Low Sun Angle):** The same energy is smeared across a larger surface area. Low efficiency $\rightarrow$ Cold.
- **Warming Time:** The longer the daytime duration, the less time nighttime has to cool down the surface, making it hotter in general.

### Key Orbital Events (Solstices & Equinoxes)

These represent the four specific points in Earth's orbit where the sun is at a $90^\circ$ angle to a specific latitude.

| **Event**           | **Month** | **Direct Sun Latitude (90∘ Angle)**  | **Result (Northern Hemisphere)** |
| ------------------- | --------- | ------------------------------------ | -------------------------------- |
| **Spring Equinox**  | March     | $0^\circ$ (Equator)                  | Equal Day/Night                  |
| **Summer Solstice** | June      | $23.5^\circ$ N (Tropic of Cancer)    | Longest Day / Start of Summer    |
| **Autumn Equinox**  | September | $0^\circ$ (Equator)                  | Equal Day/Night                  |
| **Winter Solstice** | December  | $23.5^\circ$ S (Tropic of Capricorn) | Shortest Day / Start of Winter   |

For the Southern Hemisphere, just swap the results, for example:
On the Winter Solstice, it is technically Summer for the Southern Hemisphere. Therefore summer happens on December.

---

### Regional Exceptions

- **The Equator:** Consistent concentration of rays year-round. No "seasons" in the traditional sense; just constant heat.
- **The Poles:** Extreme tilt leads to the **Midnight Sun** (Sun never sets) or **Polar Night** (Sun never rises).

> [!INFO]- Miscellaneous Trivia
> - **Distance Myth:** Earth is actually closer to the sun in January (Perihelion) than in July (Aphelion), proving distance doesn't cause seasons.
> - **Orbit Speed:** Earth orbits the sun at **approximately 100,000km/h**
> - **Terminology:** "Solstice" comes from Latin _sol_ (sun) and _sistere_ (to stand still).
> - **Opposite Seasons:** Because the tilt is fixed in space, the Northern and Southern hemispheres always experience inverted logic.
> - **ISS:** Astronauts on the ISS appear weightless. They are in constant free fall due to orbiting Earth, similar to conditions with zero gravity.
